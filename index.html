Chapter 1 — Worker Insolvency: The BAI

The Benefit Adequacy Index (BAI) answers a single question: can a laid-off worker still pay rent? BAI = Max WBA ÷ Weekly Housing Cost. Below 1.0 is systemic failure — the benefit cannot cover housing alone, forcing workers to choose between rent and every other survival expense.

Chapter 2 — The Corporate Tax Escape: WBI

The Regressive Wage Base Index (WBI) exposes the employer side of the equation. WBI = Taxable Wage Base ÷ Avg Annual Wage. Employers pay SUI tax only on wages up to the statutory cap — a cap that has been frozen for decades while wages rose. The lower the WBI, the more regressive the system: high earners' wages above the cap are entirely exempt.

Figure 2 — WBI stagnation. Maryland frozen at $8,500 since 1992. Virginia at $8,000 since 2010.
DC raised its base once in 2018 but remains below its historical ratio.
Methodology note: The taxable wage base is the dollar cap on which employers pay SUI tax. Maryland: $8,500 (frozen since 1992). Virginia: $8,000 (frozen since 2010). DC: $9,000 (raised in 2018). Average wages have grown 40–80% in the same period. A wage-indexed base would be $11,866 in Maryland, $11,389 in Virginia, and $15,286 in DC.

Chapter 3 — The Poverty Trap: MIPI

The Multi-Income Penalty Index (MIPI) quantifies what happens when a UI recipient tries to supplement their income with part-time work. MIPI = (Earnings − Disregard) ÷ Max WBA. High MIPI means the system claws back nearly the entire value of supplementary earnings — punishing resourcefulness and trapping workers in full dependency.

Chapter 4 — The Trust Fund Shortfall

If the SUI taxable wage base had kept pace with average wage growth since 2010, employers would contribute significantly more per worker — and state trust funds would be solvent enough to support adequate benefits. The gap between what employers pay and what a wage-indexed system would require is the structural shortfall.

Methodology & Data Sources

Data Sources

  • BLS QCEW: Average annual wages, covered employment (2024 annual average)
  • State DOL statutes: SUI taxable wage bases, maximum weekly benefits
  • DOL UI Financial Data: Historical average effective SUI tax rates by state and year
  • HUD Fair Market Rents: Weekly housing cost proxies by jurisdiction
  • FEC API (v1): Campaign finance data, 2024 cycle, Schedule A itemized contributions
  • Census ACS 2022: Median household income by congressional district
  • Congress.gov: Committee assignments, member metadata (119th Congress)

Index Formulas

  • BAI = Max Weekly Benefit ÷ Median Weekly Housing Cost  |  Below 1.0 = systemic failure
  • WBI = Taxable Wage Base ÷ Average Annual Wage
  • MIPI = (Earnings − Disregard Threshold) ÷ Max Weekly Benefit
  • Housing Gap = Median Weekly Housing − Max Weekly Benefit
  • Employer Gap = (Expected Base − Statutory Base) × DOL Effective Rate × Covered Employment

Honest Limitations

  • Campaign finance categorization uses keyword matching on contributor names — approximate. Official FEC industry codes would be more precise.
  • Self-funding detection uses last-name matching and may include false positives.
  • Employer gap uses the 2010-ratio method (Method B). Alternative methodologies produce different dollar figures — see source code for scenarios A–E.
  • BLS wage and employment data are 2024 annual averages; 2026 figures are projected.
  • Committee assignments reflect the 119th Congress (2025–2027) and change each Congress.
